üéØ What is Pricing & Why Does It Matter?

Pricing is picking how much money to ask for when you sell something you made. It's super important because you want to make sure you get MORE money back than you spent making it!

üåü The Big Rule: Always charge MORE than what you spent. If you spent $3 making something, you need to sell it for more than $3!

Think of it like this: If you use $5 of ingredients to make cookies and sell them for $4, you LOSE $1. That's like giving away your allowance! üò±

Pricing is deciding how much money to charge for your product. It's one of the MOST important decisions you'll make as a business owner!

The Golden Rule: Your price must be HIGH enough to cover all your costs AND leave you with profit. Price too low = lose money. Price too high = no one buys!

Here's the scary truth: If it costs you $5 to make something and you sell it for $4, you LOSE $1 every time someone buys. The more you sell, the more money you lose! üò±

Pricing strategy is the process of determining the optimal price point for your products or services. It directly impacts your profitability, market positioning, and long-term business sustainability.

The Fundamental Principle: Your price must cover all direct costs (materials, labor, packaging), indirect costs (overhead, fees, marketing), AND generate profit for reinvestment and personal income.

Understanding the relationship between price, volume, and profit margin is essential. A low price with high volume can work, but only if your margins remain positive. Most handmade businesses succeed with higher prices and moderate volume.

üìñ Business Words You Need to Know

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
The total cost of materials and supplies needed to make your product.
üí° Example: Flour + eggs + peanut butter for dog treats = $16.27
Markup
The extra amount you add on top of your costs. Usually shown as a percentage or multiplier.
üí° Example: A 100% markup (or 2x) means you double your cost. If it costs $5, you sell for $10.
Profit
The money left over AFTER you pay for all your costs. This is YOUR money to keep!
üí° Example: Sell for $10, costs $5 = $5 profit
Profit Margin
The percentage of your selling price that is profit.
üí° Example: $5 profit on a $10 item = 50% profit margin
Wholesale Price
The lower price you'd charge when selling in bulk to stores (so THEY can resell it).
üí° Example: You sell to a pet store for $4/bag so they can sell for $7/bag.
Retail Price
The full price customers pay when buying directly from you.
üí° Example: At a farmers market, customers pay $7/bag directly to you.
Overhead
Hidden costs beyond materials: electricity, tools, marketing, your time, booth fees, etc.
üí° Example: Your Etsy fees, business cards, and the mixer you use to make treats.
Break-Even Point
The number of sales needed to cover all your startup and operating costs‚Äîno profit, no loss.
üí° Example: You spent $100 on supplies. At $5 profit per item, you break even after selling 20 items.
ROI (Return on Investment)
The percentage of profit you make compared to how much you invested.
üí° Example: Invested $50, made $75 back = 50% ROI (you earned $25 profit on $50 invested).

ü§î Things to Consider When Pricing

üí∞ What are your competitors charging?

Look at what others sell similar products for. If everyone sells dog treats for $8-12 per bag, pricing yours at $25 might be too high (unless yours are super special!). But also don't go too low‚Äîif you're at $3 when others are at $8, customers might think something is wrong with your product.

‚úÖ Action: Search Etsy, local markets, and stores to see what similar products cost.

üéØ Who is your customer?

A dog owner who shops at Whole Foods and buys organic food for their pet will pay MORE for "premium organic dog treats" than someone shopping at a dollar store. Know your customer!

‚úÖ Action: Think about where you'll sell and who will buy. Farmers markets and boutiques = can charge more. School fundraiser = keep it affordable.

‚ú® What makes your product special?

If you can explain WHY your product is worth more, you CAN charge more! Things that add value: organic ingredients, handmade with care, custom orders, local ingredients, unique flavors/designs, eco-friendly packaging.

‚úÖ Action: Make a list of 3-5 things that make your product special. Use these in your marketing!

üì± Where will you sell?

Different selling places have different fees that eat into your profit:

  • Etsy: ~10-12% in fees
  • Farmers Market: $20-100 booth fee per day
  • Craft Fairs: $25-200 table fee
  • Instagram/Direct: Payment processing ~3%
  • Consignment stores: They keep 30-50% of sales

‚úÖ Action: Add selling fees to your costs BEFORE calculating your price!

üì¶ Do you want to sell wholesale?

If a store wants to carry your product, they'll want to buy at HALF your retail price (so they can mark it up and make money too). This means your retail price needs to be at least 2x your cost to sell wholesale AND make profit.

üí° Example: If it costs you $3 to make, sell retail at $8. Stores buy at $4, you still profit $1, and they sell at $8.

üéÅ Can you offer deals and discounts?

Customers LOVE discounts! But you can only offer them if your regular price has enough profit built in. A 20% off sale is great marketing‚Äîbut only if you're not losing money!

‚úÖ Action: Set your price high enough that you can offer 10-20% off and still make profit.

üö´ Common Pricing Mistakes (Don't Do These!)

‚ùå Mistake #1: Not Paying Yourself
Your time is valuable! If you spend 2 hours making treats, that's 2 hours you could be doing something else. Always include your hourly rate as a cost.
‚ùå Mistake #2: Forgetting Hidden Costs
It's not just ingredients! Don't forget: electricity, gas to buy supplies, wear on your tools, labels, bags, business cards, and selling platform fees.
‚ùå Mistake #3: Racing to the Bottom
Trying to be the cheapest is a losing game. Someone can always undercut you. Instead, focus on being the BEST and charge accordingly.
‚ùå Mistake #4: Emotional Pricing
Don't price based on what YOU would pay or what feels "fair." Price based on your ACTUAL costs plus profit. The math doesn't lie!
‚ùå Mistake #5: Never Raising Prices
When your supply costs go up, your prices should too! Review your pricing every 6 months.

‚úÖ Your Pricing Checklist

Before you set your price, make sure you can check off each of these:

  • I've added up ALL my material/supply costs
  • I've calculated my time at a fair hourly rate ($10-15+ minimum)
  • I've included packaging costs per item
  • I've considered selling platform fees (Etsy, market booth, etc.)
  • I've researched what competitors charge
  • My price is at least 2x my total costs
  • I would make a profit if I offered a 20% discount
  • I could sell to stores at half my retail price and still profit
  • My price feels right for my target customer
  • I've tested my price with a few sales

üìà

üìù

Keep a notebook or spreadsheet of every purchase. You'll need this for pricing AND taxes!
üó£Ô∏è
Ask for Feedback

üéØ
